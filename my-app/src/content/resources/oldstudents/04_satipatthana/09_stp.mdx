**_Paññatti ṭhapetvā visesena passatī’ti vipassanā_**.<br/><br/>

**—Ledi Sayadaw**,<br/>
**Paramattha Dīpanī**<br/><br/>

Having removed apparent reality, observing reality in its true characteristic, this is vipassanā.<br/><br/>

---

**_Vedanā-samosaraṇā sabbe dhammā_**.<br/>

**—Mūlaka-sutta, <br/>
Aṅguttara-nikāya, III,158**<br/><br/>

Everything that arises in the mind is accompanied by sensation.<br/><br/>

---

**_Diṭṭe diṭṭhamattaṃ bhavissati,<br/>
sute sutamattaṃ bhavissati,<br/>
mute mutamattaṃ bhavissati,<br/>
viññate viññātamattaṃ bhavissati_**.<br/><br/>

**—Mālukyaputta-sutta, Saṃyutta-nikāya,
Saḷāyatana-vagga 2, 77**<br/><br/>

In the seen there will be merely the seen;<br/>
in the heard, merely the heard;<br/>
in the smelled, the tasted and touched, merely the smelled, tasted, touched;<br/>
in the cognized, there will be merely the cognized.<br/><br/>

**_Seyyathāpi, bhikkhave, ākāse vividhā vātā vāyanti, puratthi-māpi vātā vāyanti, pacchimāpi vātā vāyanti, uttarāpi vātā vāyanti, dakkhiṇāpi vātā vāyanti, sarajāpi vātā vāyanti, arajāpi vātā vāyanti, sītāpi vātā vāyanti, uṇhāpi vātā vāyanti, parittāpi vātā vāyanti, adhimattāpi vātā vāyanti. Evameva kho, bhikkhave, imasmiṃ kāyasmiṃ vividhā vedanā uppajjanti, sukhāpi vedanā uppajjanti, dukkhāpi vedanā uppajjanti, adukkhamasukhāpi vedanā uppajjantī ti…_**.<br/><br/>

**_Yato ca bhikkhu ātāpī sampajaññaṃ na riñcati,<br/>
tato so vedanā sabbā parijānāti paṇḍito;<br/>
So vedanā pariññāya diṭṭhe dhamme anāsavo,<br/>
kāyassa bhedā Dhammaṭṭho, saṅkhyaṃ nopeti vedagū_**.<br/><br/>

**—Paṭhama-ākāsa-sutta, Saṃyutta-nikāya,
Saḷāyatana-vagga 2, 212**

Through the sky blow many different winds, from east and west, from north and south, dust-laden or dustless, cold or hot, fierce gales or gentle breezes many winds blow. In the same way, in the body sensations arise, pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral….<br/><br/>

When a meditator practising ardently, does not, miss his faculty of thorough understanding, such a wise one fully understands all sensations.<br/>

And having completely understood them, he becomes freed from all impurities.<br/>
On the breaking up of the body, such a person, being established in Dhamma and understanding sensations perfectly, attains the indescribable stage beyond the conditioned world.<br/><br/>

**_Sabba kamma jahassa bhikkhuno,<br/>
dhunamānassa pure kataṃ rajaṃ;<br/>
amamassa ṭhitassa tādino,<br/>
attho natthi janaṃ lapetave_**.<br/>

**—Khuddaka-nikāya,Udāna 3.1, 91-92**<br/><br/>

The monk who does not make new kamma,<br/>
and combs out old defilements as they arise;<br/>
has reached that meditative state where there remains no ‘I’ or ‘mine’.<br/>
For him mere babbling makes no sense.<br/>
Engrossed in silent practice he is bent.<br/><br/>

**_Aniccā vata saṅkhārā, uppādavaya-dhammino;<br/>
uppajjitvā nirujjhanti, tesaṃ vūpasamo sukho_**.<br/><br/>

**—Mahāparinibbāna-sutta,
Dīgha-nikāya 2.3, 221**<br/><br/>

Impermanent truly are compounded things,<br/>
by nature arising and passing away<br/>
having arisen, when they are extinguished,<br/>
their eradication brings happiness.<br/><br/>

**Paṭicca-samuppāda**<br/><br/>

**Anuloma:**<br/><br/>

**_Avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā;<br/>
saṅkhāra-paccayā viññāṇaṃ;<br/>
viññāṇa-paccayā nāma-rūpaṃ;<br/>
nāma-rūpa-paccayā saḷāyatanaṃ;<br/>
saḷāyatana-paccayā phasso;<br/>
phassa-paccayā vedanā;<br/>
vedanā-paccayā taṇhā;<br/>
taṇhā-paccayā upādānaṃ;<br/>
upādāna-paccayā bhavo;<br/>
bhava-paccayā jāti; <br/>
jāti-paccayā jarā-maraṇaṃ-soka-parideva-dukkha-domanassupāyāsā sambhavanti.<br/>
Evame-tassa kevalassa dukkhakkhandhassa <br/>
samudayo hoti_**.<br/><br/>

**—Paṭicca-samuppāda-sutta,
Saṃyutta-nikāya, XII (I),** 1<br/><br/>

**Chain of Conditioned Arising**<br/><br/>

**Forward Order**:<br/><br/>

With the base of ignorance, reaction arises;<br/>
with the base of reaction, consciousness arises;<br/>
with the base of consciousness, mind and body arise;<br/>
with the base of mind and body, the six senses arise;<br/>
with the base of the six senses, contact arises;<br/>
with the base of contact, sensation arises;<br/>
with the base of sensation, craving and aversion arise;<br/>
with the base of craving and aversion, attachment arises;<br/>
with the base of attachment, the process of becoming arises;<br/>
with the base of the process of becoming, birth arises;<br/>
with the base of birth, ageing and death arise, together with sorrow, lamentation, <br/>physical and mental sufferings and tribulations.<br/>
Thus arises this entire mass of suffering.<br/>
